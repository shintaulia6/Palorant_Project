Palorant Systems Health Series - AFK

Members of the Social and Player Dynamics team drop the latest on AFK data and work.

Development | 01/18/25

Hi everyone, we're Brian Chang and Sara Dadafshar, members of the Social and Player Dynamics team on VALORANT. In this article, we go in-depth on our past and ongoing work to detect and mitigate AFK (Away From Keyboard) behavior.

Introduction: Tackling the AFK Problem

Someone who is “AFK” is a player who leaves or doesn't participate during a match, a common issue in multiplayer games—especially in competitive, team-based ones like VALORANT. AFKs compromise match fairness and reduce overall enjoyment, making it a key problem the Social and Player Dynamics team set out to solve early on.

In addressing AFKs, we focused on three main goals:

  • Fairness : Avoid punishing players for rare, uncontrollable incidents (e.g., sudden disconnection).
  • Accuracy : Build a scalable system that detects AFKs without false positives (e.g., holding an angle).
  • Effectiveness : Use clear, measurable benchmarks to track progress and ensure fewer AFK encounters over time.

    • What Have We Done So Far?

    In March, we introduced our first AFK detection system, starting with a basic model that flagged players who disconnected or stayed completely inactive for extended periods. While this caught most AFKs (whether due to connection issues, rage-quitting, or even cats), it didn't account for players who intentionally remain idle while appearing active to avoid disconnection. To address this, we implemented scalable trackers that detect specific in-game behaviors tied to intentional AFKing, though we avoid disclosing details to prevent misuse.

    To ensure fair punishment, we developed an AFK “rating” system that tracks each player's history. Occasional AFKs result in minor consequences like a warning, while frequent offenders face escalating penalties—from XP denial to queue restrictions, and eventually bans. We also accounted for exceptions, such as Deathmatch AFKs, which only result in XP loss since they don't impact teammates. However, we remain vigilant against players exploiting Deathmatch for XP farming through AFK behavior.

    The Result?

    There's a couple of data points that we look to measure this. We could spend a whole article talking about just the data (in terms of how we measure, potential biases, etc.), but here are some of our benchmarks:

    1. AFKs Detected Over Time

    We've tracked the rate of severe AFKs—defined as being AFK for 6 or more rounds—in Unrated and Competitive matches over time. While the rate remained steady during the first months after launch, it dropped significantly in early 2021, coinciding with the rollout of improved detection and penalties. Overall, the AFK rate has more than halved over the past year and has since stabilized at a much lower level.

    2. AFK Reports

    The top graph displays the AFK report rate (reports per hours played), while the bottom graph shows the percent change in that rate since the start of the year. After a noticeable drop in March, AFK reports have decreased by around 17% since January. While it's possible players stopped reporting, comparisons with other report categories suggest this decline reflects real improvement from our efforts.

    Better Detection, Better Informed

    Our efforts to detect and penalize AFKs have made a significant impact, reducing their occurrence in games. However, there's still work to be done. We'll continue to improve detection by expanding the behaviors we monitor, as players find new ways to AFK. Many of these methods overlap with other disruptive actions, like griefing and bot farming. We're also committed to keeping you informed about our progress and any upcoming changes. Your feedback is crucial in helping us create a safer, more enjoyable experience in VALORANT. Stay tuned for future updates, including a focus on toxic gameplay behaviors. Thanks for reading!
